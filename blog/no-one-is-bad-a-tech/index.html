No one is bad at tech

04 Mar, 2024 | 3 min read

I popped into my local library the other day. I needed to print a delivery label for something, and I’d long since given up on owning a printer. I mean, why have a huge chunk of plastic taking up space, for the two times a year you need to print something, when it constantly thinks it’s out of ink, or needs its drivers replacing, takes and age to start up, never has the…

I’m digressing. Let’s start again…

I went to the library the other day to print something. It took me forever. Why? Was the library busy? Did the computers run slowly? Was there no one around to help with the infernally slow system?

Nope. None of that happened.

It took so long because I took so long. I couldn’t understand how the system worked. I’d have been out of there quicker if I’d asked any of the three staff around for help. But no, I am a tech expert (and also a stubborn man), so I did not ask for help. It’s just a networked printer for goodness’ sake. How hard could it be?

Anyway, 15 minutes later I gave in and asked for help. They couldn’t have been nicer when they showed me how absurdly easy everything was. They even pointed out that I didn’t need to use the library computer at all. If I connected my phone to the WiFi and scanned the QR code on the printer, I could print straight from my device.

I guess I have two points here.

The first is this: support your local library. They’re great. Your mileage is going to vary, but in my case, if I need to print something, I know it’ll cost me 20p and take less than 3 minutes. (Or at least, it will next time).

Everyone struggles with technology. Everyone.

My second point is this: everyone struggles with technology. Everyone. I’ve worked in tech for well over a decade. I’ve run technology transformation projects in hospitals. I’ve organised tech conferences, hackathons, and workshops across the country. I’ve been coding since I was 15 years old and have built dozens of websites from the ground up for just about every use case you could imagine. I currently manage the technology for one of the UK’s largest private foundations. And, it took me 20 minutes to print something at my local library. Everyone struggles with tech.

Not only does technology move so fast, but the requirements in any given case are so different. I set up the printing at my office, so in theory, I shouldn’t have had an issue. But the expectation on how that works is completely different. 40-something known people, printing from pre-approved devices, inside a private building, is a very different ask from a public building with potentially 100s of people a day needing to print from any number of devices. A pastry chef works in the kitchen, but they might not be the best person in the building to cook you a beef Wellington. (Note to self: rewatching The Bear always makes for better metaphors.)

To reiterate, there was nothing wrong with how the library was set up. It was intuitive, functioned quickly, and did exactly what I needed it to do. It was, with perhaps only a little hyperbole, perfect.

My issue was that I just wasn’t familiar with the system, and I immediately laid my assumptions of how it “should” work on top of it.

Not all technology is built with all people in mind.

I’m certainly not bad when it comes to tech. But I think that’s my point - I don’t believe anyone is inherently bad at technology. It’s just that a lot of tech is designed only considering a certain type of mindset. I have that mindset. But when I encounter a system that is specifically built to account for people who aren’t nerds who don’t think in that way, I need to take a step back.

No one is inherently bad at technology. It’s just that not all technology is built to help all people equally. We need to get better at that.
